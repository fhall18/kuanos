# What is a harmful algal bloom?
Algal blooms are a natural phenomenon that occur when populations of algae rapidly increase in water bodies like oceans, bays, and freshwater systems. They become harmful when they involve toxin-producing species or grow dense enough to cause environmental damage. Not all blooms are toxic — some are just a nuisance — but the ones that are can be seriously dangerous to humans, pets, livestock, fish, and wildlife. University of New Hampshire
Cyanobacteria and blue-green algae — what's the difference?
They're the same thing, just with different names. Cyanobacteria (also known as blue-green algae) are a type of photosynthetic bacteria that, like algae, derive their energy from photosynthesis. They're not technically algae at all — they're bacteria — but they behave so similarly that the name stuck. They often (but not always) discolor water bright green or blue-green and form a surface scum. VtNOAA Ocean Service
Do all HABs produce the same toxins? No — it depends on the organism and the environment.
There are two main categories:
Freshwater HABs are dominated by cyanobacteria. Some cyanobacteria produce cyanotoxins such as microcystin, cylindrospermopsin, and anatoxins, which cause a range of health effects from minor to life-threatening. The most common in the US are microcystins — liver toxins that can cause gastrointestinal illness in humans and mortality in pets, livestock, and wildlife. VtNOAA Ocean Service
Marine (saltwater) HABs are mostly caused by different organisms entirely. Red tides are caused by a specific dinoflagellate, Karenia brevis, which produces brevetoxin — a toxin that affects the central nervous system of fish and marine wildlife. Other common marine toxins include saxitoxin, domoic acid, ciguatoxins, and okadaic acid. Some of these cause shellfish poisoning syndromes (neurotoxic, amnesic, diarrhetic) in humans who eat contaminated seafood. UF/IFAS Extension Miami-Dade Countycdc
A wild example of the two colliding: in 2018 in Florida, cyanobacterial-laden freshwater from Lake Okeechobee was released down the Caloosahatchee River at the same time that red tide was gathering along the coast, potentially exposing residents to a mixture of both microcystin (liver toxin) and brevetoxin (neurotoxin) simultaneously. Phys.org
What causes them?
The main drivers are warm water, lots of sunlight, and nutrient pollution — particularly nitrogen and phosphorus. This is mainly caused by nutrient pollution from agricultural runoff, with contributions from municipal wastewater discharge and a boost from climate change. Heavy rainfall and storms can also spike blooms by stirring up nutrient-rich sediment and flushing more runoff into water bodies. Clean Water Action
Are they getting worse because of climate change? Yes.
Toxic cyanobacteria thrive at warmer temperatures, with growth accelerating at water temperatures above 25°C. As surface waters warm, the water column increasingly stratifies along a temperature gradient, which further favors cyanobacterial dominance. For example, warming temperatures in Lake Erie have contributed to large HABs that now last into the early winter months, and in recent years such blooms have been found more often and in more places across the United States. Rising CO₂ levels, sea-level rise, and drought-driven salinity increases are all compounding the problem. ScienceDirectCDC
A few other useful facts:

HABs cost the US an estimated $82 million per year in impacts to fishing and tourism industries. ScienceDirect
Some species release neurotoxins that become airborne, causing direct respiratory impacts on nearby humans and animals — even people on the beach who never touch the water. Beachapedia
Under federal law, NOAA is primarily responsible for marine and Great Lakes HABs, while the EPA handles freshwater HABs. US EPA
Visually, blue-green algae blooms are often described as looking like pea soup or spilled green paint, while red tides have a rusty red appearance. mass

The bottom line: HABs are a broad umbrella term, cyanobacteria/blue-green algae are the main freshwater culprit, and different organisms produce very different toxins with very different health effects. Climate change is making all of it worse.
