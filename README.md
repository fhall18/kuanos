# Harmful Algal Blooms (HABs)

## What Is a Harmful Algal Bloom?

Algal blooms are a natural phenomenon that occur when populations of algae rapidly increase in water bodies like oceans, bays, and freshwater systems. They become *harmful* when they involve toxin-producing species or grow dense enough to cause environmental damage. Not all blooms are toxic — some are just a nuisance — but the ones that are can be seriously dangerous to humans, pets, livestock, fish, and wildlife.

---

## Cyanobacteria and Blue-Green Algae — What's the Difference?

They're the same thing, just with different names. Cyanobacteria (also known as blue-green algae) are a type of photosynthetic bacteria that, like algae, derive their energy from photosynthesis. They're not technically algae at all — they're bacteria — but they behave so similarly that the name stuck. They often (but not always) discolor water bright green or blue-green and form a surface scum.

---

## Do All HABs Produce the Same Toxins?

No — it depends on the organism and the environment. There are two main categories:

### Freshwater HABs (Cyanobacteria)
Dominated by cyanobacteria, these blooms produce **cyanotoxins** such as microcystin, cylindrospermopsin, and anatoxins, which cause a range of health effects from minor to life-threatening. The most common in the US are **microcystins** — liver toxins that can cause gastrointestinal illness in humans and mortality in pets, livestock, and wildlife.

### Marine (Saltwater) HABs (Red Tides)
Mostly caused by dinoflagellates and diatoms. Red tides are caused by the dinoflagellate *Karenia brevis*, which produces **brevetoxin** — a toxin that affects the central nervous system of fish and marine wildlife. Other common marine toxins include saxitoxin, domoic acid, ciguatoxins, and okadaic acid. Some of these cause shellfish poisoning syndromes (neurotoxic, amnesic, diarrhetic) in humans who eat contaminated seafood.

> **Notable example:** In 2018 in Florida, cyanobacterial-laden freshwater from Lake Okeechobee was released down the Caloosahatchee River at the same time that red tide was gathering along the coast — potentially exposing residents to a mixture of both microcystin (liver toxin) and brevetoxin (neurotoxin) simultaneously.

---

## What Causes HABs?

The main drivers are warm water, lots of sunlight, and nutrient pollution — particularly nitrogen and phosphorus. Primary sources include:

- Agricultural runoff
- Municipal wastewater discharge
- Heavy rainfall and storm events (which stir up nutrient-rich sediment)
- Climate change (warming water temperatures)

---

## Are HABs Getting Worse Due to Climate Change?

Yes. Toxic cyanobacteria thrive at warmer temperatures, with growth accelerating at water temperatures above 25°C. As surface waters warm, the water column increasingly stratifies, which further favors cyanobacterial dominance. For example, warming temperatures in Lake Erie have contributed to large HABs that now last into the early winter months, and in recent years such blooms have been found more often and in more places across the United States.

Rising CO₂ levels, sea-level rise, and drought-driven salinity increases are all compounding the problem.

---

## Key Facts

- HABs cost the US an estimated **$82 million per year** in impacts to fishing and tourism industries.
- Some species release neurotoxins that become **airborne**, causing direct respiratory impacts on nearby humans and animals — even people on the beach who never touch the water.
- Under federal law, **NOAA** is primarily responsible for marine and Great Lakes HABs, while the **EPA** handles freshwater HABs.
- Visually, blue-green algae blooms are often described as looking like **pea soup or spilled green paint**, while red tides have a **rusty red** appearance.

---

## Summary

HABs are a broad umbrella term. Cyanobacteria/blue-green algae are the main freshwater culprit, while dinoflagellates drive most marine blooms. Different organisms produce very different toxins with very different health effects. Climate change is making all of it worse.
